# Cross-Domain Hypotheses — Health Data as a Universal Sensor

## Premise

25.8 million hospital admissions, 3 million death records, 24 million disease notifications, 4 million birth records, and 10 million facility snapshots — all georeferenced by municipality and time across 10 years in São Paulo. This isn't just health data. It's a distributed sensor network that captures signals about **crime, climate, economics, infrastructure, education, and urban planning** — often before purpose-built systems detect them.

Each hypothesis below uses SUS data to predict or detect something in a *different* domain.

---

## CD-1 — Violence Mapping: The "Dark Figure" of Crime

**Domain:** Public Security

**Hypothesis:** SIH external-cause admissions (ICD-10 X85-Y09 assault, X60-X84 self-harm, T74 maltreatment) provide a more complete picture of violence than police reports, especially for domestic violence and gang-related injuries where victims never file a report. The gap between hospital-recorded violence and police statistics per municipality quantifies **underreporting** — and municipalities with the largest gap have the weakest public safety trust.

**Why it matters:** Public security policy is built on police data that systematically underestimates violence. A woman beaten by her partner goes to the hospital but not to the police station. A gang member stabbed in a favela gets stitched up at the UPA but nobody files a BO. The hospital sees the truth that the police statistics miss.

**Data:** SIH (external cause codes by municipality × month) vs. SSP-SP crime statistics (publicly available).

**Feasibility:** High — all data already downloaded. External cause codes are standard in SIH.

## CD-2 — Respiratory Admissions as Air Quality Sensors

**Domain:** Environmental Monitoring

**Hypothesis:** Spikes in respiratory admissions (J44 COPD, J45 asthma, J18 pneumonia, J20-J21 bronchitis) in a municipality precede or coincide with air quality deterioration events, effectively turning the 340-municipality hospital network into a **distributed air quality monitoring system** — especially valuable for municipalities without CETESB monitoring stations.

**Why it matters:** Only ~50 of São Paulo's 645 municipalities have air quality monitoring. The other 595 are invisible. But every municipality has a hospital. If respiratory admissions spike in a municipality with no sensor, that's an air quality alert generated by the health system itself.

**Data:** SIH (respiratory ICD codes by municipality × month) vs. CETESB/QUALAR air quality data (publicly available).

**Feasibility:** High — SIH data ready, CETESB data downloadable via API.

## CD-3 — Substance Abuse as a Crime Leading Indicator

**Domain:** Public Security / Social Policy

**Hypothesis:** Municipalities where F19 (substance abuse) admissions are rising will see increased assault-related admissions (X85-Y09), traffic injuries (V01-V99), and self-harm (X60-X84) within 3-6 months. The F19 trend is a **leading indicator** of violence and accidents, detectable before crime statistics reflect it.

**Why it matters:** F19 admissions rose 76% in São Paulo over 10 years. That's not just a health problem — it's a predictor of public safety deterioration, workplace accidents, and family disintegration. Municipalities where substance abuse is surging need coordinated health + security + social assistance responses.

**Data:** SIH only — both the predictor (F19 trends) and outcomes (X85-Y09, V01-V99) are in the same database.

**Feasibility:** Very high — fully testable with existing data, no external sources needed.

## CD-4 — The Kidney Stone Epidemic as Climate Change Signal

**Domain:** Climate Adaptation / Urban Planning

**Hypothesis:** The near-doubling of N20 (kidney stone) admissions (+95%) is not uniformly distributed — it concentrates in municipalities with higher urban heat island effects and lower green coverage. Mapping N20 incidence trends by municipality produces a **health-derived heat vulnerability index** that identifies climate-stressed populations years before traditional climate vulnerability assessments.

**Why it matters:** Climate adaptation planning in São Paulo relies on temperature projections and socioeconomic indices. But kidney stones are a direct physiological response to heat and dehydration. Where stones are surging, bodies are already telling us the climate impact is real — right now, not in 2050.

**Data:** SIH (N20 by municipality × year) vs. INPE satellite temperature data / municipal green area indices.

**Feasibility:** Medium — SIH data ready, environmental data requires additional download.

## CD-5 — Avoidable Hospitalizations as Economic Productivity Loss

**Domain:** Economics / Labor

**Hypothesis:** Municipalities with high ICSAP rates suffer measurable economic productivity losses. Each avoidable hospitalization removes a working-age adult from the economy for an average of 6+ days (direct stay) plus 2-4 weeks recovery. The aggregate lost workdays per municipality per year, valued at local average wages, produces an **ICSAP economic cost index** that quantifies the return on investment of primary care improvement.

**Why it matters:** Health secretaries argue in clinical terms. Finance secretaries think in R$. Translating 3.65 million avoidable hospitalizations into lost GDP, lost wages, and lost tax revenue makes the case for primary care investment in language that treasury departments understand.

**Data:** SIH (ICSAP admissions, age, length of stay, municipality) + IBGE economic data (municipal GDP, avg wages).

**Feasibility:** High — SIH data ready, IBGE economic data freely available.

## CD-6 — Dengue Clusters as Sanitation Infrastructure Failures

**Domain:** Urban Planning / Sanitation

**Hypothesis:** Municipalities with persistent dengue recurrence (SINAN data showing high incidence across multiple years, not just epidemic peaks) have **structural sanitation deficits** — inadequate drainage, irregular water supply forcing residents to store water, and insufficient waste collection. The persistent dengue map is a proxy for a sanitation infrastructure failure map.

**Why it matters:** Dengue vector control is reactive — spray when cases spike. But persistent recurrence means the *breeding conditions* haven't been fixed. Our SINAN data spanning 2016-2025 can distinguish "one bad year" municipalities from "chronically infested" ones. The chronically infested ones need civil engineering, not fumigation trucks.

**Data:** SINAN (dengue notifications by municipality × epi_week, 2016-2025) vs. SNIS sanitation data (publicly available).

**Feasibility:** High — SINAN data ready, SNIS data downloadable.

## CD-7 — Obstetric Migration as Infrastructure Decay Detector

**Domain:** Health Infrastructure Planning / Political Science

**Hypothesis:** When pregnant women start delivering in a different municipality than where they live (SINASC: CODMUNRES ≠ CODMUNNASC), it's the earliest detectable signal that local healthcare infrastructure is failing. An increasing obstetric migration rate predicts **facility closures, physician departures, or quality deterioration** in the municipality of origin — typically 6-18 months before it appears in CNES registries.

**Why it matters:** Pregnant women are the most sensitive "quality sensors" in any health system — they research, compare, and travel when they perceive local care as inadequate. Their behavior reveals infrastructure decay before any official metric captures it. This turns SINASC into a real-time infrastructure quality monitor.

**Data:** SINASC (municipality of residence vs. municipality of birth, 2016-2022).

**Feasibility:** Very high — SINASC data already downloaded.

## CD-8 — Mental Health Deinstitutionalization Audit

**Domain:** Social Policy / Housing / Homelessness

**Hypothesis:** The 49% drop in psychiatric admissions (F20 schizophrenia) combined with shortened stays (24 → 19 days) represents deinstitutionalization. In municipalities where psychiatric bed closures (CNES) were NOT matched by proportional CAPS (community mental health center) expansion, we should see increases in: (a) psychiatric emergency readmissions within 30 days, (b) F19 substance abuse admissions (self-medication), (c) X60-X84 self-harm/suicide, and (d) external-cause admissions (violence involving psychiatric patients).

**Why it matters:** Deinstitutionalization is policy in Brazil since the Psychiatric Reform (Law 10.216/2001). But the promise was "close hospitals, open community services." In many municipalities, the first half happened and the second didn't. The result: 43,000 people who used to be in hospitals are now somewhere — on the streets, in prisons, or dead. This analysis would quantify where the reform succeeded and where it became abandonment.

**Data:** SIH (F20, F19, X60-X84 by municipality × year) + CNES (psychiatric beds + CAPS registrations by municipality).

**Feasibility:** High — all data already downloaded.

## CD-9 — Birth Patterns as Economic Stress Sensor

**Domain:** Economics / Demography

**Hypothesis:** Changes in SINASC birth patterns — declining birth rates, shifting cesarean-to-vaginal ratios, decreasing prenatal visit counts, increasing teenage pregnancy rates — are **leading indicators of economic stress** at the municipal level, detectable 9-12 months before traditional economic indicators. During economic downturns, fewer planned pregnancies occur, women delay or skip prenatal visits, and the proportion of births in SUS (vs. private) hospitals increases.

**Why it matters:** Economic monitoring relies on GDP, employment, and tax revenue data that arrives with significant lag. Birth patterns respond to economic stress almost immediately (decision to conceive) but manifest with a 9-month delay — creating a unique medium-term economic forecasting window. A municipality where births are dropping, prenatal visits declining, and SUS share rising is a municipality under economic pressure.

**Data:** SINASC (births, prenatal visits, delivery type, maternal age, hospital type, 2016-2022) vs. IBGE/CAGED economic data.

**Feasibility:** High — SINASC data already downloaded.

## CD-10 — Hospital Admission Patterns as Epidemic Early Warning for Other States

**Domain:** Epidemiological Surveillance / Interstate Policy

**Hypothesis:** São Paulo, as Brazil's most connected state (busiest airports, highways, and intercity bus routes), typically sees epidemic waves **before** neighboring states. The temporal lag between São Paulo's dengue/respiratory outbreak peaks (SINAN + SIH) and the same peaks in Minas Gerais, Paraná, and Rio de Janeiro follows predictable patterns based on transportation connectivity. São Paulo's outbreak data can serve as an **early warning system for the entire Southeast region**.

**Why it matters:** State health secretariats plan in isolation. But diseases don't respect state borders. If São Paulo's dengue season peaks in week 8, and historically Minas Gerais peaks in week 12 with 80% probability, then Minas has a 4-week head start to prepare — but only if someone builds the model.

**Data:** SINAN SP (already downloaded) + SINAN for MG/PR/RJ (downloadable via PySUS with the same pipeline we already built).

**Feasibility:** Medium — requires downloading SINAN data for neighboring states. Pipeline already exists.
