## Mortality Trend — The Central Finding

Mortality rose from **31.5% (2016) to 35.6% (2025)**, a +4.1 percentage point increase. The trend is not uniform:

| Year | Admissions | Deaths | Mortality |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2016 | 10,891 | 3,428 | 31.5% |
| 2017 | 10,764 | 3,338 | 31.0% |
| 2018 | 10,772 | 3,236 | **30.0%** (lowest) |
| 2019 | 10,466 | 3,290 | 31.4% |
| 2020 | 14,681 | 4,944 | 33.7% (COVID surge) |
| 2021 | 13,195 | 4,135 | 31.3% |
| 2022 | 10,968 | 3,771 | 34.4% |
| 2023 | 11,738 | 3,908 | 33.3% |
| 2024 | 11,612 | 4,321 | **37.2%** (highest) |
| 2025 | 11,287 | 4,013 | 35.6% |

Key observations:
- Pre-COVID (2016–2019): mortality hovered around 30–31%, with a slight dip in 2018
- COVID acute (2020): volume spiked 40% but mortality rose modestly to 33.7%
- Post-COVID (2022+): **mortality accelerated while volume returned to baseline** — this is the crisis
- 2024 peaked at **37.2%** — the worst year on record
- 2025 shows slight improvement (35.6%) but remains well above pre-COVID levels

## COVID Era Comparison

| Era | Admissions | Mortality | Mean LOS | Mean Age |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-COVID (2016–2019) | 44,447 | **31.1%** | 10.3 days | 41.4 |
| COVID Acute (2020–2021) | 26,322 | 32.4% | 8.2 days | **50.9** |
| Post-COVID Early (2022–H1 2023) | 17,248 | 33.2% | 9.8 days | 41.9 |
| Post-COVID Late (H2 2023–2025) | 28,357 | **36.3%** | 9.9 days | 44.5 |

The COVID era brought older patients (mean age jumped from 41 to 51), but that reversed post-COVID. The mortality increase in the post-COVID late era (+5.2pp vs pre-COVID) persists even though mean age is only slightly higher (44.5 vs 41.4). This suggests the mortality rise is **not fully explained by patient demographics** — something structural changed.

## Demographics

- **Mean age:** 44.4 years (median 53)
- **Sex:** 53.3% male, 46.7% female
- **Admission type:** **95.4% emergency** — nearly all J96 patients arrive through the ER
- **Age gradient:** Mortality rises steeply with age. The 75+ group has the highest mortality, while the <1 (neonatal) group has a different profile entirely.

The crisis is overwhelmingly an **acute** respiratory failure problem. Chronic respiratory failure (J96.1) has much lower mortality (12.8%) and represents a different clinical population. The "Other" category has the highest mortality (47.7%) — these need investigation.

## Hospital Landscape — The ICU Gap

**The single most striking finding in this overview:**

| ICU Capacity | Hospitals | Admissions | Mortality |
|---|---|---|---|
| No ICU | 442 (79%) | 59,520 | **40.6%** |
| Small ICU (1–10 beds) | 88 (16%) | 36,118 | 26.7% |
| Medium ICU (11–30 beds) | 26 (5%) | 17,752 | 22.2% |
| Large ICU (>30 beds) | 6 (1%) | 2,984 | 21.5% |

**51% of J96 patients are treated in hospitals with no ICU beds** — and their mortality is nearly double that of patients at hospitals with large ICUs (40.6% vs 21.5%). This 19-percentage-point gap is the most actionable finding so far.

By facility type:
- Hospital geral: 33.6% mortality (104,559 admissions — 90% of volume)
- Hospital especializado: 25.0% (6,114 admissions)
- Pronto socorro: 40.2% (1,028 admissions)
- Unidade mista: 46.2% (104 admissions)

## Related Respiratory Conditions (2024 vs 2016)

| Condition | Volume Change | Mortality Change |
|---|---|---|
| J96 Respiratory Failure | +7% | **+5.7pp** |
| J80 ARDS | +45% | +1.4pp |
| J44 COPD | +10% | +1.2pp |
| J18 Pneumonia | -5% | +1.5pp |

J96 has the **largest mortality increase** of all respiratory conditions by far (+5.7pp vs next highest +1.5pp). ARDS (J80) has a dramatic volume increase (+45%) but comparatively modest mortality change. This suggests J96's mortality crisis is specific to how these patients are managed, not a broad respiratory care degradation.

## Comorbidity Impact

Top mortality-amplifying comorbidities:

| Comorbidity | Prevalence | Mortality With | Mortality Without | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Stroke | 0.9% | 70.8% | 32.6% | **+38.1pp** |
| Sepsis | 2.4% | 68.7% | 32.1% | **+36.6pp** |
| Kidney Failure | 2.7% | 64.2% | 32.1% | **+32.1pp** |
| Pulmonary Fibrosis | 0.2% | 59.1% | 32.9% | +26.2pp |
| Heart Failure | 1.7% | 56.4% | 32.6% | +23.8pp |
| Diabetes | 2.6% | 55.1% | 32.4% | +22.7pp |
| Hypertension | 5.1% | 49.1% | 32.1% | +17.0pp |

Asthma is **protective** (-25.4pp) — asthma-related respiratory failure typically affects younger patients and is more reversible.

Note: comorbidity prevalence is low overall (mean 0.22 per patient), suggesting **significant under-coding** of secondary diagnoses. The true comorbidity burden is likely much higher.

## Data Quality Notes

1. **ICU marker (MARCA_UTI):** Shows 0% ICU usage when filtered to values 1–6, but 407,892 ICU days (UTI_MES_TO) are recorded. The MARCA_UTI encoding appears to use different codes than expected. This needs investigation in notebook 04 (ICU capacity). ICU days data is usable even if the binary marker is unreliable.

2. **Pre-2016 records:** A small number of records (847) have admission dates before 2016 (billing lag in 2016 parquet). These are excluded from analysis by filtering to year >= 2016.

3. **Comorbidity under-coding:** Mean comorbidity count of 0.22 is implausibly low for a critical care population. Secondary diagnosis fields are likely underutilized.

---

## Key Takeaways for Downstream Notebooks

1. **The crisis is real and worsening** — mortality rose +4.1pp (2016–2025), with the worst year being 2024 (37.2%)
2. **Post-COVID structural effect** — mortality accelerated after COVID even as volume normalized and patient age returned near baseline
3. **The ICU gap is massive** — 19pp mortality difference between no-ICU and large-ICU hospitals. Over half of patients go to no-ICU hospitals.
4. **Nearly all emergencies** — 95.4% arrive via ER, limiting opportunities for planned intervention
5. **Comorbidity amplifiers** — sepsis, kidney failure, and stroke dramatically increase mortality but are infrequent (or under-coded)
6. **J96 is unique** — its mortality increase is 4x larger than any other respiratory condition
