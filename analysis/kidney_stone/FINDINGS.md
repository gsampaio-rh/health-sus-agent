# The Kidney Stone "Epidemic" — Not What You Think

## TL;DR

Kidney stone (N20) hospital admissions in São Paulo doubled from 14,307 (2016) to 32,111 (2025) — a **+124% increase**, the largest volume surge in the entire 25.8 million-admission SIH dataset. The obvious hypothesis is climate change (heat → dehydration → stones). **The data says otherwise.**

The "epidemic" is **62% technology adoption, 38% organic growth**. A single new procedure — transureteroscopic ureterolithotripsy — was incorporated into SUS by the Ministry of Health in December 2020 (Portaria SAES nº 1.127) and went from **zero admissions to 11,146 per year** in just 4 years. 93 municipalities now offer it. The rest of the growth (+46.5%) is real but slow — driven by aging, obesity, and dietary changes.

This is not an epidemic. It's a **technology diffusion success story** — more people getting *treated*, not more people getting *sick*.

## 2. The Regulatory Trigger

**March 2019:** Portaria SCTIE nº 15 — Ministry of Health incorporates transureteroscopic ureterolithotripsy into SUS (recommended by CONITEC).

**December 2020:** Portaria SAES nº 1.127 — Procedure formally added to SIGTAP table as code **04.09.01.059-6**.

**2021:** First year of adoption — 55 municipalities, 1,987 procedures.

**2025:** 93 municipalities, 11,146 procedures, 34.7% of all kidney stone admissions.

## 4. The Seasonality Myth

**Summer vs Winter difference: +0.6%**

There is **no meaningful seasonal pattern** in kidney stone admissions. All 12 months show nearly identical volumes. This directly contradicts the naive "heat = dehydration = stones" hypothesis.

If climate were the primary driver, we'd expect a 20-40% summer peak (as seen in US/European studies). Instead, the growth is uniform across seasons, pointing to **structural factors** (technology, aging, diet) rather than acute environmental triggers.

| Season | Avg Monthly Admissions |
|--------|----------------------|
| Summer (Dec-Feb) | 1,683 |
| Autumn (Mar-May) | 1,735 |
| Winter (Jun-Aug) | 1,674 |
| Spring (Sep-Nov) | 1,791 |

## 5. WHO — Demographics

### Sex: Women Are Catching Up

Women now account for **~54%** of kidney stone admissions, up from ~52% in 2016. Both sexes are growing, but female admissions grew faster. This aligns with global trends linking rising female kidney stone rates to obesity and metabolic syndrome.

### Age: The 40-59 Explosion

The 40-49 and 50-59 age groups saw the largest absolute increases, becoming the dominant cohorts by 2025. The 60-69 group also grew significantly. This is consistent with:
- Population aging
- Peak age for metabolic stone disease
- Increased access to diagnosis (CT scans) in this age range

## 8. What's Really Happening — The Multi-Causal Story

The "epidemic" has **three overlapping causes**:

### A. Technology Adoption (62% of growth)
A new minimally invasive procedure was incorporated into SUS in 2020 and rapidly diffused from 0 to 93 municipalities. This captured patients who previously:
- Were treated conservatively (pain meds, wait)
- Were referred to São Paulo capital
- Never sought treatment at all

### B. Genuine Increase in Stone Disease (38% of growth)
The +46.5% organic growth in traditional procedures reflects real increases driven by:
- **Obesity epidemic:** BMI is the strongest modifiable risk factor for kidney stones. Brazil's obesity rate nearly doubled from 2003 to 2020.
- **Dietary shifts:** Ultra-processed food consumption, high sodium, low water intake.
- **Population aging:** Peak incidence is 40-60 years; São Paulo's population is aging.
- **Improved diagnosis:** More CT scans detecting stones that previously went undiagnosed.

### C. Access Democratization
Interior cities that previously couldn't treat kidney stones locally now have the equipment and trained urologists. Patients who would have traveled to São Paulo capital (or gone untreated) are now receiving care closer to home. This is a health equity improvement.

## 9. Why the Climate Narrative Is Wrong (But Not Completely)

The absence of seasonality doesn't mean climate has zero effect — it means climate is not the **primary driver** of the observed growth. Possible reconciliation:

- **Chronic dehydration** (not acute heat events) may contribute year-round
- **Urban heat island** effects are persistent, not seasonal
- Climate effects may be real but dwarfed by the technology and obesity signals
- The climate signal might emerge in a finer-grained analysis (daily temperature × admissions)

## 12. Why Are Centers Slow? — Root Cause Diagnosis

We drilled into the hospital-level data (CNES) for each underperforming center to find the actual cause.

### Guarulhos (4.8 days) — Conservative management, not surgery

**83% of patients get clinical/conservative treatment** (procedure 0305020021 — pain meds + wait for the stone to pass), averaging 5.2 days. The main hospital (CNES 2082861, 117 patients) does NOT perform the new procedure at all. Average cost is R$521 — consistent with no surgery being done. Even when the new procedure is used (22 patients), it averages 3.1 days — twice as slow as Piracicaba. **Fix:** Train urologists, shift protocol to surgical first-line for eligible stones.

### Osasco (4.4 days) — Emergency/complex cases

One hospital (CNES 0008028) handles 81% of patients at 4.8 days. ICU usage is 6.1% (highest of any center), mortality is 1.5% (also highest). They use endoscopic procedure 0415010012 at 5.2 days — the same code that São Carlos uses at 1.0 days. **Fix:** Protocol optimization and case severity triage.

### Botucatu (3.5 days) — Teaching hospital effect

All 466 patients go to UNESP Botucatu, a university teaching hospital. Teaching hospitals systematically have longer stays due to training protocols. New procedure is used at 33% but averages 2.9 days (vs 1.5d at Piracicaba). Their traditional surgery averages 4.8 days. **Fix:** Academic protocol review, benchmark against Piracicaba.

### Sorocaba (2.9 days) — Split performance within one city

3 fast hospitals (1.5-1.8d avg, 52-99% new procedure) coexist with 2 slow hospitals (2.8-3.3d, 40-45% new procedure). The 2 slow hospitals handle **79% of volume** (2,358 of 2,965 patients). Same city, same patient population, 2x difference in outcomes. **Fix:** Within-city routing to fast hospitals + slow hospital benchmarking.

### São Carlos (1.2 days) — The gold standard, WITHOUT the new procedure

Only 2 patients out of 970 got ureterolithotripsy. São Carlos is fast because of excellent operational processes: their traditional surgery (procedure 0409010170) averages 1.7 days — vs 4.8 days at Botucatu for the same code. One hospital (CNES 2080931), one team, tight protocols. **This proves that speed is about hospital processes, not which procedure is used.**

### The Smoking Gun: Same Procedure, 4x Speed Difference

Procedure 0409010170 (traditional surgical removal) at different centers:

| Center | Avg Stay | Difference vs Best |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| São Carlos | 1.7 days | baseline |
| Franca | 2.3 days | +35% |
| Santo André | 2.2 days | +29% |
| Sorocaba | 3.6 days | +112% |
| Presidente Prudente | 3.3 days | +94% |
| Botucatu | 4.8 days | +182% |
| Guarulhos | 8.0 days | +371% |

The same surgery takes 1.7 days in São Carlos and 8.0 days in Guarulhos. The difference is not technology — it's operational protocols, staffing, post-op management, and discharge criteria.

Statewide: Elective admissions average 1.5d stay, emergency admissions average 2.9d.

## 14. Policy Implications (ML-Backed)

All recommendations below are derived from the SHAP analysis and ML simulation, not assumptions.

1. **Create elective urology pathways (biggest lever — saves 4,199 bed-days/yr).** Emergency admission is the #1 SHAP driver (21% of model importance). Converting 30% of ER admissions to scheduled at 72 high-ER hospitals frees 11.5 beds and prevents 11 deaths per year. This is an organizational change, not a technology investment.

2. **Standardize treatment protocols (saves 772 bed-days/yr).** Hospital conservative treatment culture is SHAP #2 (8% importance). The 32 hospitals that default to >40% conservative management keep ALL patients longer — regardless of individual procedure. Reducing this culture to 20% frees 2.1 beds.

3. **Combined: 5,171 bed-days, 14 beds, R$2.4M, 11 deaths prevented per year.** The two interventions are non-additive due to SHAP interactions — emergency admission and conservative culture amplify each other.

4. **Replicate the Piracicaba outpatient lithotripsy model.** Piracicaba processes 35% of patients as same-day outpatient (procedure 0409010146, 95% 0-day discharge). No slow center has this. The lithotripsy specialty has the strongest speed signal in the model (SHAP r=-0.96).

5. **Don't just buy equipment — fix processes.** ML finding: more surgical beds → LONGER stays (SHAP=0.197, r=+0.75). More patient volume → SHORTER stays. Infrastructure doesn't help; experience does. São Carlos proves this: 1.2d avg stay with 0% new procedure.

6. **Prevention opportunity:** The +46.5% organic growth is preventable through hydration campaigns, dietary guidance, and obesity prevention programs.

### Key ML Findings

**1. Emergency admission is the #1 driver (21% of model importance)**
Emergency patients stay 0.62 SHAP-days longer on average. This is a patient-flow problem, not a clinical one. Creating scheduled urology pathways would shift the admission mix.

**2. Hospital "culture" matters as much as equipment**
Hospitals with high conservative treatment rates (SHAP=0.235) keep patients longer regardless of the individual patient's procedure. This is institutional behavior — how the hospital operates, not what it has.

**3. Bigger hospital ≠ faster — volume helps, beds don't**
More surgical beds → LONGER stays (SHAP=0.197, r=+0.75). But higher patient volume → SHORTER stays (SHAP=0.161). Experience drives speed, not infrastructure.

**4. Lithotripsy specialty routing is the strongest "speed signal"**
Patients routed to lithotripsy specialty (ESPEC 09) have the strongest negative correlation with stay (r=−0.96). This is the single most reliable way to shorten stays in the data.

**5. The new procedure helps, but it's not the biggest lever**
Ureterolithotripsy (SHAP=0.043, r=−0.87) shortens stays, but its impact is smaller than emergency admission rate, hospital culture, and age.

### SHAP Interactions

The model reveals non-obvious **interaction effects**:

- **Emergency × Hospital Culture**: Emergency patients are penalized MORE at hospitals with a conservative treatment culture. The combination of emergency admission + high-conservative hospital produces longer stays than either factor alone.
- **Age × Procedure Type**: Older patients are penalized MORE when receiving traditional surgery vs newer procedures. The age effect is amplified by procedure choice.
- **Volume × ER Rate**: Higher hospital volume helps more at high-emergency-rate hospitals — experience with emergency workflows matters.

### Why These Numbers Are Trustworthy

1. **Model-based, not rule-based.** Previous simulations used manual formulas (e.g., "shift X patients, save Y days"). This simulation uses the trained ML model to predict each patient's stay under counterfactual conditions.

2. **Conservative assumptions.** We only convert 30% of ER admissions (not all). We only reduce conservative culture to 20% (not zero). Real implementation could do more.

3. **Interaction effects captured.** The SHAP analysis shows emergency admission and conservative culture amplify each other. The combined intervention captures this non-linearity.

4. **Cost grounded in real data.** R$468/bed-day is the median SUS reimbursement from 63,104 actual admissions, not an estimate.

5. **Mortality differential from observed data.** Emergency mortality (0.54%) vs elective mortality (0.12%) is measured from the same 63,104 patients, not assumed.

## Methodology

- **Data source:** SIH/SUS (Sistema de Informações Hospitalares), São Paulo state, 2016-2025
- **Filter:** Primary diagnosis (DIAG_PRINC) starting with "N20" (ICD-10 kidney/ureteral calculus)
- **Total records analyzed:** 206,500 kidney stone admissions out of 25,787,169 total SIH records
- **Deep dive dataset:** 63,104 N20 admissions (2024-2025) for controlled comparisons
- **CNES cross-reference:** 17 hospitals matched against facility registry for equipment, bed counts, and service capabilities
- **Statistical methods:** Pearson correlation, multiple linear regression (R²=0.578), controlled comparisons by procedure × admission type × sub-diagnosis
- **ML model:** LightGBM regressor trained on 63,104 patient-level observations with 29 features, evaluated via 80/20 train-test split. SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) used for feature importance and interaction analysis.
- **Procedure identification:** PROC_REA field matched against SIGTAP codes; code 0409010596 identified as transureteroscopic ureterolithotripsy via Portaria SAES nº 1.127/2020
- **Municipality classification:** SP Metropolitan Area (14 core municipalities) vs Interior
